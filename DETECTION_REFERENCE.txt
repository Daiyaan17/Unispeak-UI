                    UNISPEAK — DETECTION REFERENCE GUIDE

This document lists all expressions/gestures the app can detect, explains how
braille characters work, and describes how the CV pipeline detects them.

================================================================================

classifies gestures using geometric rules (which fingers are extended, angles,

STABILITY BUFFER:
  - The app collects predictions over 12 consecutive frames

 PART 2: HOW BRAILLE CHARACTERS WORK
================================================================================

Braille is a tactile writing system using raised dots arranged in cells.
Each braille cell has 6 dot positions in a 3-row × 2-column grid:

    ┌───┬───┐
    │ 1 │ 4 │   ← Top row
    ├───┼───┤
    │ 2 │ 5 │   ← Middle row
    ├───┼───┤
    │ 3 │ 6 │   ← Bottom row
    └───┴───┘

A character is defined by WHICH dots are raised (present).
With 6 positions, there are 2^6 = 64 possible combinations.

 PART 3: HOW THE APP DETECTS BRAILLE CHARACTERS
================================================================================

The braille detection uses a computer vision pipeline with OpenCV:

STEP 1: IMAGE PREPROCESSING
──────────────────────────
    Camera Frame (RGB)
         │
         ▼
    Convert to Grayscale
         │
         ▼
    Gaussian Blur (7×7 kernel)
    — smooths noise, preserves dot edges
         │
         ▼
    Adaptive Threshold (Gaussian, block=15, C=8)
    — converts to black/white, adapts to local lighting
    — dots become white blobs on black background
         │
         ▼
    Morphological Open (3×3 ellipse kernel)
    — removes tiny noise specks
         │
         ▼
    Morphological Close (3×3 ellipse kernel)
    — fills small gaps in dot contours


STEP 2: DOT DETECTION
─────────────────────
    Binary Image
         │
         ▼
    Find Contours (external only)
         │
         ▼
    For each contour, calculate:
      • Area — must be between 30 and 2000 pixels
      • Circularity = 4π × Area / Perimeter²
        — must be > 0.55 (close to circular)
      • Center point via image moments
      • Radius = √(Area / π)
         │
         ▼
    Filter: Keep only contours that are:
      ✓ Right size (not too big, not too small)
      ✓ Circular enough (rejects lines, squares, etc.)
         │
         ▼
    Output: List of dot center coordinates (x, y) and radii


STEP 3: CLUSTERING DOTS INTO CELLS
──────────────────────────────────
    Dot Centers
         │
         ▼
    Calculate nearest-neighbor distances between all dots
    — uses scipy.spatial.distance.cdist
         │
         ▼
    Find median spacing between dots
    — this tells us how far apart dots within a cell are
         │
         ▼
    Define cell boundaries:
      cell_width  = 2.5 × median spacing
      cell_height = 3.5 × median spacing
         │
         ▼
    Greedy clustering:
    — Pick an unused dot as seed
    — Find all unused dots within cell_width and cell_height
    — Group them as one cell (max 6 dots per cell)
    — Repeat until all dots are assigned


STEP 4: MAPPING DOTS TO BRAILLE POSITIONS
─────────────────────────────────────────
    For each cell (group of dots):
         │
         ▼
    Find the center of the cell (midpoint of all dots)
         │
         ▼
    For each dot in the cell:
      — Is it LEFT or RIGHT of center? → Column 1 (dots 1,2,3) or Column 2 (dots 4,5,6)
      — Is it in TOP, MIDDLE, or BOTTOM third? → Row determines exact position
      — Map to braille dot index:
          Left column:  Top=1, Middle=2, Bottom=3
          Right column: Top=4, Middle=5, Bottom=6
         │
         ▼
    Output: Tuple of active dot indices, e.g. (1, 2) for letter B


STEP 5: DECODING TO TEXT
────────────────────────
    Dot pattern tuple
         │
         ▼
    Look up in BRAILLE_TO_CHAR dictionary
    Example: (1, 2) → 'b'
             (1, 3, 4) → 'm'
             (1, 2, 3, 4, 5) → 'q'
         │
         ▼
    Concatenate decoded characters from all cells
         │
         ▼
    Stability buffer (8 frames, 50% agreement)
    — prevents flickering, confirms detection


STEP 6: VISUAL FEEDBACK ON SCREEN
──────────────────────────────────
    On the camera feed, the app draws:
    • Cyan circles around each detected dot
    • Green dot at each dot center
    • Green rectangle around each braille cell
    • Decoded letter above each cell
    • Info bar at bottom showing full decoded text
    • Status bar showing dot count, cell count, and confidence %


================================================================================
 PART 4: HOW SIGN LANGUAGE DETECTION WORKS
================================================================================

STEP 1: HAND DETECTION
─────────────────────
    Camera Frame (RGB, mirrored)
         │
         ▼
    MediaPipe HandLandmarker model (float16 TFLite)
    — Detects hand presence in frame
    — Extracts 21 landmark points per hand:

         Landmark Map:
              4 (thumb tip)
             /
            3 (thumb IP)
           /
          2 (thumb MCP)        8 (index tip)     12 (middle tip)
         /                    /                  /
        1 (thumb CMC)    7   /              11  /    16 (ring tip)  20 (pinky tip)
        |               /   /              /   /    /              /
        0 (WRIST) --- 5 ---6          9 --10  13--14--15      17--18--19
                       |              |        |               |
                       └──────────────┴────────┴───────────────┘
                                  (palm base)


STEP 2: LANDMARK NORMALIZATION
─────────────────────────────
    21 raw landmarks (x, y, z in 0-1 range)
         │
         ▼
    Subtract wrist position (landmark 0)
    — makes all coords relative to the wrist
         │
         ▼
    Divide by max distance from wrist
    — normalizes hand size (works at any distance from camera)


STEP 3: FINGER EXTENSION CHECK
──────────────────────────────
    For each finger, determine if it's "extended" (open) or "curled" (closed):

    THUMB:
      — Calculate palm center = average of wrist(0), index_mcp(5), pinky_mcp(17)
      — If thumb tip(4) is farther from palm center than thumb MCP(2) × 1.1
        → Thumb is EXTENDED

    INDEX, MIDDLE, RING, PINKY:
      — If fingertip Y-coordinate < PIP joint Y-coordinate
        (tip is ABOVE the second knuckle)
        → Finger is EXTENDED

    Result: [thumb, index, middle, ring, pinky] = [True/False × 5]


STEP 4: GESTURE CLASSIFICATION
──────────────────────────────
    The 5-boolean finger state + landmark distances are used to match rules:

    Example: fingers_up = [False, True, True, False, False]
    — Only index and middle are up → "Peace ✌️"

    Example: fingers_up = [True, True, False, False, True]
    — Thumb, index, and pinky up → "I Love You 🤟"

    Rules are checked in priority order (specific gestures first,
    generic fallbacks last).


STEP 5: STABILITY & OUTPUT
─────────────────────────
    Raw prediction (every frame, ~30 FPS)
         │
         ▼
    Push to 12-frame circular buffer
         │
         ▼
    If 70%+ of buffer agrees on same gesture:
      → CONFIRMED — append to sentence
      → Update translation panel
    Otherwise:
      → Keep showing raw prediction but don't commit

    Confirmed gestures accumulate into a sentence:
      "Thumbs Up 👍" → "Peace ✌️" → "Hello / Stop ✋"
      Sentence: "Thumbs Up 👍 Peace ✌️ Hello / Stop ✋"

 PART 5: HOW TO PLACE AN IMAGE FOR BRAILLE DETECTION
================================================================================

STEP-BY-STEP SETUP:

  1. PRINT OR PREPARE YOUR BRAILLE
     — Print a braille sheet on white paper (black dots on white)
     — Or open a braille image on your phone/tablet screen
     — High contrast is key: dark dots, light background

  2. POSITION THE BRAILLE IN FRONT OF THE CAMERA

         ┌──────────────┐
         │   CAMERA     │
         │   LENS       │
         └──────┬───────┘
                │
                │  15-30 cm (6-12 inches)
                │
         ┌──────▼───────┐
         │  ● ●         │
         │  ●   ●       │  ← Braille paper/screen
         │  ●   ●       │     held FLAT facing camera
         │              │
         └──────────────┘

  3. ANGLE: Hold the paper FLAT and PARALLEL to the camera
     — Do NOT tilt the paper
     — The camera should look straight at the surface

         ✅ CORRECT:              ❌ WRONG:
         Camera                   Camera
           │                        │
           │  (perpendicular)       │  (angled)
           ▼                         \
         ▬▬▬▬▬▬ paper               ╲ paper tilted

  4. DISTANCE:
     — Start at about 15-20 cm (6-8 inches)
     — Move closer if the dots are small
     — Move farther if only partial text is visible
     — The dots should appear as clear circles on screen

  5. LIGHTING:
     — Use even, diffused lighting (overhead room light)
     — Avoid direct sunlight or harsh shadows
     — No light source shining directly at the camera

         ✅ GOOD:                 ❌ BAD:
         💡 (overhead)            💡──→ camera (glare)
           │                         
           ▼                      Shadow across paper
         paper ← evenly lit

  6. WHAT YOU'LL SEE ON SCREEN:
     — Cyan circles appear around detected dots
     — Green rectangles show identified braille cells
     — Decoded letters appear above each cell
     — Bottom bar shows the full decoded text
     — Top-left shows: "⠿ X dots · Y cells  Z%"

  7. HOLD STEADY for 1-2 seconds
     — The stability buffer needs consistent frames
     — Once confirmed, text appears in the translation panel


EXAMPLE PLACEMENT FOR A BRAILLE WORD "HELLO":

    Braille for H-E-L-L-O on paper:

    ●·  ●·  ●·  ●·  ●·
    ●●  ·●  ●·  ●·  ·●
    ··  ··  ●·  ●·  ●·

    Hold this 15cm from camera → app should decode "hello"


USING A PHONE/TABLET AS BRAILLE SOURCE:
  — Search "braille alphabet image" online
  — Display it on your phone at full brightness
  — Hold the phone screen facing your laptop camera
  — Keep the phone steady and parallel to the camera


================================================================================
 TIPS FOR BEST DETECTION
================================================================================

SIGN LANGUAGE:
  • Hold your hand 30-60 cm from the camera
  • Use a plain, non-busy background (solid wall)
  • Ensure good lighting (avoid backlighting)
  • Keep fingers clearly separated for spread gestures
  • Hold each gesture steady for ~1 second for confirmation

BRAILLE:
  • Use printed braille on white/light paper
  • Hold the paper flat, facing the camera
  • Ensure even lighting (no shadows across the dots)
  • Get close enough that dots are clearly visible
  • Avoid tilting — keep the paper parallel to the camera
  • Works best with high-contrast dots (dark dots on light surface)
